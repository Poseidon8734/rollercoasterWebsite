History

The oldest roller coasters are known as Russian mountains also known as Russian ice slides. The slides were generally 20-24 meters tall, consisted of 50 degree drops and were reinforced by wooden supports. Many historians believe that the first modern roller coaster was built by the French. The Promenades Aeriennes in Parc Baujon and the Les Montagnes Russes Ã  Belleville (The Russian Mountains of Belleville) were both constructed in Paris in 1817 and both featured wheeled cars securely locked to the track, guide rails to keep them on course, and higher speeds.

In 1827, a mining company in Summit Hill, Pennsylvania constructed the Mauch Chunk gravity railroad, an 8.7-mile (14.0 km) downhill track used to deliver coal to Mauch Chunk (now known as Jim Thorpe), Pennsylvania. By the 1850s, the "Gravity Road" (as it became known) was providing rides to thrill-seekers for 50 cents a ride. Railway companies used similar tracks to provide amusement on days when ridership was low.

Opening in 1901, there was one of the first looping roller coaster in Coney Island. It was made of steel and had a small loop that accompanied the ride. It came and went, by the name of Loop the Loop.

During 1934, a new attraction arrives at Cedar Point. It's name, The Tumbling Bug. It's made of steel and located in Cedar Point, an amusement park near Lake Erie. It's an example of many rides we see today as "kiddie rides".
